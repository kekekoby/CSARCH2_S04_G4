      <span class="slide__eyebrow">Shared Bus Problem</span>
      <h1>Purpose of a Bus Arbiter</h1>

      <p>
        A computer bus is a communication channel carrying data, addresses, and control
        signals between different components (Tanenbaum &amp; Austin, 2012). The bus is
        shared between multiple devices, and only one device can drive it at a time. If two
        devices try to transmit simultaneously, their signals corrupt each other, creating
        what is known as a <strong>bus conflict</strong> (Patterson &amp; Hennessy, 2013).
      </p>

      <p>
        A bus arbiter is a hardware unit that works towards preventing these bus conflicts
        from happening. Devices signal to the bus arbiter that they would like to use the
        bus by sending a Bus Request (<strong>BR</strong>) line, and the arbiter responds
        with a Bus Grant (<strong>BG</strong>) line to one device at a time (Stallings,
        2016). The device that receives the BG will then assert Bus Busy (<strong>BBSY</strong>)
        to signal that the bus is in use. When the device is finished transferring data, it
        lifts the BBSY.
      </p>

      <div class="card">
        <h3>Why this matters</h3>
        <p>
          This method not only ensures that bus conflicts will not occur, but also that
          the bus is used in an orderly manner and all requests are handled systematically.
        </p>
      </div>
