        Design Trade-offs
      </h1>

      <p>
        Prior to examining specific hardware implementations or the interactive
        simulator, it is essential to establish that mediating shared bus access
        involves deeper systemic challenges. The arbitration process is governed
        by three fundamental design considerations.
      </p>

      <div class="card">

        <h3>
          Fairness vs. Urgency Trade-off
        </h3>

        <p>
          System architects must balance equitable access among all devices
          against the prioritization of critical operations. Fairness prevents
          starvation, while prioritizing urgency guarantees responsiveness to
          time-sensitive operations (Stallings, 2016).
        </p>

      </div>

      <div class="card">

        <h3>
          Centralized vs. Distributed Decision-Making
        </h3>

        <p>
          Arbitration logic can be implemented through a single dedicated
          hardware unit (centralized) or dispersed across multiple devices on
          the bus (distributed). Centralized architectures simplify control
          logic but introduce a single point of failure. Distributed
          architectures improve fault tolerance but require more complex
          coordination protocols (Null &amp; Lobur, 2014).
        </p>

        {/* Animated Diagram */}
        <CentralizedDistributedAnimation />

      </div>

      <div class="card">

        <h3>
          The Hidden Cost: Arbitration Overhead
        </h3>

        <p>
          The arbitration process consumes system clock cycles: devices assert
          request lines, the arbiter evaluates priorities, and grant signals
          propagate back. During this evaluation phase, the bus remains idle.
          This delay is arbitration latency (Patterson &amp; Hennessy, 2013).
        </p>

      </div>
