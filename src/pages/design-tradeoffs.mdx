    <div class="card">
      <h3>Fairness vs. Urgency Trade-off</h3>
      <p>
        System architects must balance equitable access among all devices
        against the prioritization of critical operations. Fairness prevents
        starvation, while prioritizing urgency guarantees responsiveness to
        time-sensitive operations (Stallings, 2016).
      </p>
    </div>

    <div class="card">
      <h3>Centralized vs. Distributed Decision-Making</h3>
      <p>
        Arbitration logic can be implemented through a single dedicated
        hardware unit (<strong>centralized</strong>) or dispersed across
        multiple devices on the bus (<strong>distributed</strong>).
        Centralized architectures simplify control logic but introduce a
        single point of failure. Distributed architectures improve fault
        tolerance but require more complex coordination protocols
        (Null &amp; Lobur, 2014).
      </p>
      <p>
        Use the toggle below to compare both approaches. Watch how requests

      </p>

    <div class="card">
      <h3>The Hidden Cost: Arbitration Overhead</h3>
      <p>
        The arbitration process consumes system clock cycles: devices assert
        request lines, the arbiter evaluates priorities, and grant signals
        propagate back. During this evaluation phase, the bus remains idle.
        This delay is arbitration latency (Patterson &amp; Hennessy, 2013).
      </p>
    </div>
